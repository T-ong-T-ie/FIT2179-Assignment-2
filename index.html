Climate and Dengue: Links Across Malaysia

Dengue is an important public health concern in tropical areas. This dashboard shows how three simple climate factors—humidity, temperature and sunlight—relate to dengue cases across Malaysian states.

Investigator: HU LONGXI  •  Monash University Malaysia  •  May 2026

Line Chart: Humidity vs Dengue (2014–2019)
A line chart with two vertical axes showing average humidity (teal) and dengue case totals (purple). It helps show how humidity and case numbers move over time.
Key finding (explained): The chart shows a clear pattern where dengue case increases often match times when humidity falls. One explanation is the "Flushing Effect": during very wet monsoon periods heavy rain can wash away mosquito breeding sites, while in slightly drier months standing water and human water storage can create places for mosquito larvae to grow. This, together with higher temperatures in drier months, can lead to more outbreaks.

Phase III

Combined Risk Overview

This section brings different data together in one chart so you can see how climate and dengue cases are linked. Each state in each year is shown as a point so we can compare conditions. The chart highlights that higher temperatures often match larger outbreaks, even when humidity is not at its highest. (Here "mosquito" is used instead of the technical term "vector".)

Bubble Chart: Temperature vs Humidity
X: Temperature, Y: Relative Humidity. Bubble size shows number of cases and color shows the state.
What this shows (plain): The big bubbles cluster mostly in the lower-right area of the chart. That means many large outbreaks happen when temperatures are higher but humidity is not at its peak. Higher temperatures can speed up how quickly the virus becomes infectious inside a mosquito. This time is called the extrinsic incubation period (EIP): the time a mosquito needs after biting an infected person before it can pass the virus on. Shorter EIP at higher temperatures can help outbreaks grow faster.
